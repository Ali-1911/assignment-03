BLUE WHALE

OVERVIEW:

The Blue Whale (Balaenoptera musculus) is the largest mammal on Earth. These magnificent creatures can grow up to 100 feet long and weigh as much as 200 tons. Despite their size, Blue Whales feed on one of the smallest organisms in the ocean â€” krill. They can consume up to 4 tons of krill each day during feeding season. Blue Whales are known for their loud, low-frequency calls, which can travel long distances underwater.

HABITAT:

Blue Whales are found in all major oceans except the Arctic. They prefer deeper waters and are often found migrating between feeding grounds in polar regions and breeding grounds in tropical and subtropical waters.

BEHAVIOR & DIET

Blue Whales are solitary animals, though they occasionally form small groups. Their diet consists almost exclusively of krill, and they feed by taking in large amounts of water and filtering it through their baleen plates.

CONSERVATION

Blue Whales are classified as endangered. They were heavily hunted during the 20th century, and while they are now protected, their population is still recovering. Climate change, pollution, and ship strikes remain significant threats.

Prey and Predators

Prey

Blue Whales feed almost exclusively on krill, tiny shrimp-like crustaceans found in large swarms in cold ocean waters. During feeding season, Blue Whales can consume up to 4 tons of krill per day. They use their baleen plates to filter huge volumes of water, trapping the krill inside their mouths.

Predators

Due to their enormous size, Blue Whales have very few natural predators. However, Orcas (also known as killer whales) are known to occasionally attack Blue Whale calves, though these attacks are rare. Human activities, such as ship strikes and entanglement in fishing gear, pose a much greater threat to Blue Whales than any natural predator.
